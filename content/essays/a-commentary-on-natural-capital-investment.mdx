## 1. Natural Capital and Ecosystem Services

### 1.1 Defining Natural Capital

The impact investment fund Gresham House defines natural capital as the "stock of renewable and non-renewable natural resources that combine to provide a flow of benefits to people and planet." The firm continues, explaining that there are four core ways through which nature delivers these benefits to people and planet:

- **Provisioning** – the direct material outputs from nature, including timber and energy.
- **Regulating** – the indirect benefits generated through the regulation of ecosystems, including carbon sequestration and pollination services.
- **Supporting** – the fundamental ecological processes which support the delivery of other ecosystem services, including enhanced soil quality.
- **Cultural** – the non-material benefits from nature, including recreational and aesthetic value.

In my view, natural capital represents the holistic intrinsic value of a natural asset, including the flows of ecosystem functions, biodiversity, and the supply of non-renewable resources within the asset. Each element individually does not capture the entirety of natural capital, and it is essential that a natural capital practitioner does not lose sight of the holistic — and unfortunately complex — systems-level concept that is natural capital.

### 1.2 The Biology of Natural Capital

Although Charles Darwin himself discussed how biodiversity, ecosystem function, and environmental change might affect one another, in what is now termed the "tangled bank theory," up until the 1970s it was broadly assumed that environmental change was the key effector in mediating biodiversity change and alterations to ecosystem function. However, it then became apparent that a significant correlation was developing between biodiversity loss and environmental change. Ecology subsequently underwent a paradigm shift as scientists pulled together new theory to explain this apparent new relationship between ecosystem function, environmental change, and biodiversity. Since this point, a wealth of research has emerged to back this new theory, producing our current understanding of this interplay — and importantly, these three dimensions form the holistic unit of natural capital, so it is essential to understand how each affects the other in order to produce a systems-level understanding.

In 2012, Cardinale et al. published a seminal paper demonstrating the direct implications biodiversity loss had on ecosystem functioning. Initially, they demonstrated a positively saturating curve, wherein an increase in biodiversity, measured as species richness, led to an incrementally decreasing benefit on productivity, measured as biomass production per unit area. This led them to question whether some species were redundant. However, upon expanding their investigation, they demonstrated that every species plays an integral role in mediating ecosystem functions — by sampling more areas of an ecosystem, at more times of year, and measuring more ecosystem functions, every species was shown to influence a key ecosystem function.

Biodiversity forms a core component of natural capital, since it influences many of the non-renewable and renewable resource flows that occur within an ecosystem. Yet natural capital represents much more, and while economics can currently account for natural resources (e.g. timber, water, and land use), it cannot yet capture whole ecosystems and the services derived from their healthy functioning (e.g. pollination, erosion control, water purification).

However, papers are emerging which outline potential approaches for modelling individual components of natural capital, which, when combined, can produce a more holistic measure for biologically relevant natural capital. For instance, a study by Wilebore et al. used a SWAT (Soil and Water Assessment Tool) analysis to model the vertical water balance of 30m x 30m cells on the Evenlode catchment, Oxfordshire, calculating the contribution of runoff to peak flow based on vegetation type, soil type, topography, and climate, in order to identify optimal vegetation placement to reduce flooding risk. Increasingly, software tools offer a potential holistic measure for natural capital itself, or at least some weighted metric across a broad selection of ecosystem services (e.g. SEED, InVEST).

## 2. The Role of Investors in Enabling Natural Capital Growth

### 2.1 The Financial Opportunity of Natural Capital

I currently observe trends of corporates and asset owners engaging with climate and natural capital purely from the perspective of mitigating risk and, more recently, with the aim of improving resilience. This adaptability piece, although important, leaves a gap — a lack of engagement, perhaps due to the quantitative ineffability of natural capital, with the potential opportunity in natural capital as an investable asset offering financial returns. I am therefore committed to identifying the potential upside of reformed natural capital assets for certain profitable activities such as agriculture, biocarbon, real estate, and timber.

Importantly, there is a key distinction between an investment opportunity in natural capital and a natural capital asset that is highly valuable, and I see this as the result of two key factors. In other asset classes, a good investment opportunity often arises from the positive differential between the value you believe an asset has and the value attributed to it by the market, alongside some future catalyst which allows the market to "realise" the asset is more valuable than previously thought, driving the value — and your return — upwards. The same holds, I believe, for natural capital, wherein the investment opportunity is the result of a differential: the enhancement of a "brownfield" site into a green asset. This makes it difficult to attract finance into the preservation of pristine or virgin natural capital assets, since a goal of conservation is static preservation, which by design prevents the creation of any catalyst or growth opportunity. Related to this is the challenge of taking over a unit of natural capital and asking for funding to preserve an asset and its ecosystem services where organisations were previously benefiting for free. Together these two factors, while highlighting the difficulty of inviting capital into already-pristine assets for their protection, also demonstrate the parallels between natural capital as an asset class and equities: natural capital restoration and realisation is a valid investment opportunity, and the principal challenge in realising its value is quantifying that value in the first place — which remains elusive.

Fundamentally, the value of an asset or company comprises the sum of all its expected future cash flows, discounted to a net present value using a factor that integrates the riskiness of the company. As Aswath Damodaran outlined, this principle can be developed into a framework highlighting the key value drivers of an asset or company:

- **Revenue Growth** — faster growth leads to a higher valuation.
- **Profit Margin** — a larger margin leads to a higher valuation.
- **Riskiness** (an aggregate of operating risk and risk of failure) — lower risk leads to a higher valuation.
- **Investment efficiency** — higher efficiency leads to a higher valuation.

I personally believe that a company or asset which engages with and utilises natural capital will drive improvement across these four variables, ultimately lending itself to a higher valuation. This builds on Damodaran's thesis of "good" companies, extending it to sustainable companies, and more specifically those which engage positively with natural capital.

First, a company that can demonstrate a genuine commitment to protecting and enhancing natural capital may be able to establish a brand premium. This can affect both B2B and B2C business, ultimately driven by customers factoring ecological impact into their purchasing decisions. For instance, an agricultural firm which uses regenerative agriculture and integrates natural capital — across soil health and water quality — across all its activities may be favoured by consumers over companies which deplete these assets, translating into market share gains and accelerated revenue growth. As demand for transparency around sustainability impacts increases, companies that position themselves as genuine stewards of natural capital — greenwashing risks aside — can capture the market's future orientation.

Second, engagement with natural capital can foster long-term growth in profitability. Initially, an asset owner's decision to work positively with natural capital might appear to shrink profit margins, whether through sourcing verified, deforestation-free materials from ecologically responsible suppliers or investing in resource-efficient technology to reduce water or energy use. But this short-term pressure is offset by longer-term profitability drivers. As sustainable production becomes the standard method, the unit cost of a premium input falls, further enhanced by the rising cost of poor practice as climate risks stack up. Businesses which actively manage natural capital are also able to insulate themselves from future financial shocks, seeing lower regulatory and legal costs as governments commit to reducing ecological footprints (e.g. the EU Deforestation Regulation) — making proactive stewardship an insurance policy against litigation and mandated remedial action.

Another financial advantage of natural capital engagement is its impact on a firm's or asset's cost of capital. Lenders recognise that a firm with robust natural capital stewardship is inherently less risky. For example, a real estate asset that uses strategic nature-based solutions to quantifiably reduce flood risk presents a lower risk profile than one failing to engage with physical risks, translating into lower interest rates and a lower cost of debt. Similarly, investors are increasingly aware that natural capital performance is a proxy for long-term viability and management quality, driving increased demand for nature-positive stock and thus lowering the cost of equity. Combined, organisations which treat natural capital as a genuine asset may experience a lower overall cost of capital, increasing their intrinsic value — compounded by the lower failure risk that natural-capital-positive organisations enjoy, since environmental catastrophes, loss of assets due to ecological collapse, and government phase-outs are demonstrably lower than for those that index on resource extraction and lack stewardship.

### 2.2 Making Natural Capital Profitable

Building on what I outlined earlier, creating an investment opportunity out of a natural capital asset will likely reside in its uplift. I believe there is significant value in the differential between a disregarded natural asset and a healthy, functioning one. In theory, one could model the financial value of the entirety of the ecosystem services offered within an asset, outline the highest-impact activities enabling uplift, and, by realising this differential, eventually own an asset worth significantly more than what it was originally worth.

For example, an unused brownfield site might carry a relatively low valuation. Identifying the ecological improvements that would have the disproportionately largest impact on the holistic natural capital valuation metric would streamline operations toward realising a greater valuation for the asset. In theory, a higher valuation would then invite buyers or act as collateral against which the asset owner could borrow capital for other financing activities. In this scenario, the value of the asset increases as a result of cross-disciplinary action combining predictive modelling, consulting work, conservation expertise, and an understanding of the core biology and underlying economics.

Perhaps the most tangible example is the treatment of a useful natural asset as a core piece of infrastructure. Regenerative agriculture or continuous cover forestry, integrated with an understanding of the ecological integrity underpinning such methods, could pay for upfront investment through long-term, predictable cash flows — provided the cost of operationalising regenerative agriculture is lower than the cost of running (or continuing to run) an intensive, monoculture-type agricultural asset.

### 2.3 The "Standards Tragedy of the Commons"

Much of the work on valuing natural capital assets continues to rely on policy or regulatory frameworks. The intangibility of natural capital and some of its core components, along with the unclear direct pathway to demonstrate reliable, long-term value for investors, highlights the nascency of the natural capital market. Across the various professionals I have spoken to, the average predicted runway for making natural capital mainstream — a core component of institutional investors' decisions — is around 10 to 15 years.

Although many challenges must be overcome to reach this point, one is, I believe, frequently overlooked. Institutions and organisations, both private and public, are each trying to develop their own standard globally, attempting to "outcompete" other standard-creators and become the leader in the space. This is observed across sustainability and ESG stakeholders more broadly. The difficulty organisations face in complying with strict ESG benchmarks ultimately incentivises poorer and poorer standards for firms to benchmark against, given the competition between standard-creators to lead in the space. For example, InVEST might offer one way of measuring natural capital, while the EU might outline a separate accounting approach entirely. Ultimately, this competition prevents consensus from being reached, and — while potentially challenging to prevent — it acts as a barrier to the creation of globally agreed, top-down mandated regulation for a natural capital valuation methodology that encapsulates all biologically relevant metrics and functions.

## 3. Vision for Natural Capital Investment

Looking forward, and hopefully in the years before the predicted runway outlined by my informal surveys, I want to see a future in which a holistic unit of nature can be reliably valued by some globally agreed standard and traded for the benefit of society and the natural world in a profitable manner. This will likely be tech-enabled, with a new wave of sustainable fintech fostering more rigorous and reliable financing of nature. It would also demand significant advancement in remote data collection technologies — also globally standardised — to build the database from which constant asset valuations can be operated; data availability remains a huge barrier to the tradability and valuation of natural assets.

Further, I want to see mapping along the entire supply chain for natural capital uplift, from investor to conservationist, where competition exists between investment funds — not standards-creators — to offer the most profitable natural capital portfolio, with value growth driven by making a real, positive change. I do believe this is all possible, with the important caveats of political and societal tailwinds and advancements in the collection, analysis, and valuation of natural capital data.

This is ambitious — very ambitious — and so I believe the best place to start is by breaking down this holistic vision into aspects that can be made profitable now, without the help of regulation. For example, focusing on flood control for one type of asset in one geography, where all variables — macroeconomic and biological — can be expertly understood to produce the most sound natural capital investment opportunity possible.
